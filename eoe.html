INTRODUCTION TO EARTH

Our home planet is the third planet from the Sun, and the only place we know of so far that’s inhabited by living things.Earth is the biggest of the four planets closest to the Sun, all of which are made of rock and metal. Earth is made up of complex, interactive systems that create a constantly changing world that we are striving to understand.we are able to observe our planet globally, using sensitive instruments to understand the delicate balance among its oceans, air, land, and life. NASA satellite observations help study and predict weather, drought, pollution, climate change, and many other phenomena that affect the environment, economy, and society.



EVOLUTION OF EARTH

The Earth was formed about 4.6 billion years ago, that's 4,600,000,000 years ago. It was formed by collisions of particles in a large cloud of material. Slowly gravity gathered together all these particles of dust and gas and formed larger clumps.As the Earth started to grow, about 4.56 billion years ago, the heavy iron sank to the center, and the lighter silicates rose to the surface. Heat generated from impacts of other bodies kept the early Earth molten.

The Formation Of The Core

The core formed during accretion, as metal from impactors sank through a magma ocean to the center of the Earth. Liquid metal in contact with liquid silicate equilibrated at high pressures and temperatures, resulting in the core and mantle compositions that we see in the Earth today.

The Formation Of The Mantle

As Earth began to take shape about 4.5 billion years ago, iron and nickel quickly separated from other rocks and minerals to form the core of the new planet. The molten material that surrounded the core was the early mantle. Over millions of years, the mantle cooled.




The Formation Of Oceanic-type Crust

Oceanic crust is constantly formed at mid-ocean ridges, where tectonic plates are tearing apart from each other. As magma that wells up from these rifts in Earth's surface cools, it becomes young oceanic crust. The age and density of oceanic crust increases with distance from mid-ocean ridges.

The Formation Of Ancient Platforms

The ancient crust the outermost shell of a terrestrial planet. Our planet’s thin, 40-kilometer (25-mile) deep crust—just 1% of Earth’s mass—contains all known life in the universe. Earth has three layers: the crust, the mantle, and the core. The crust is made of solid rocks and minerals.
